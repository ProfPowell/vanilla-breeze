The Measure

Typographers have long called the optimal line length the measure. Robert Bringhurst, in The Elements of Typographic Style, places ideal prose at 45 to 75 characters. The original Penguin paperbacks ran to about 60.

When a line exceeds 80 characters, the eye must work harder to find its return. It sweeps left, hunting for the start of the next line, and occasionally lands on the wrong one.

Multi-Column on Screen

CSS multi-column layout has existed since 2001, yet it remains conspicuously underused. The reasons are partly historical: early implementations were buggy, and horizontal scrolling felt alien on mouse-driven interfaces. Touch has changed that calculation.

But there is a deeper resistance. Designers trained on scroll-based interfaces treat the horizontal axis as navigation. To give the horizontal axis to prose is to repurpose it for a slower, more demanding use.

The best that can be said for the internet's infinite column is that it democratised publishing. The worst is that it forgot everything typography had learned in five hundred years.

When it works, the effect is disorienting in the best possible way. The text feels substantial. Each page has weight.

Pagination and Flow

The page, unlike the scroll, imposes a contract. When a reader reaches the bottom of a page and turns it, something cognitive happens: a small act of completion, a breath between clauses.

Studies of reading behaviour consistently show better retention when text is consumed in finite, bounded chunks than when it scrolls continuously.

This may explain the success of e-readers. The Kindle's paginated interface produces measurably different reading behaviour. Users slow down. Rereading rates increase.
