The History of Hypertext

The concept of hypertext predates the World Wide Web by decades. Vannevar Bush first described a system of linked information in his 1945 essay "As We May Think"[1], imagining a device called the Memex that would allow users to follow trails of associated ideas.

Ted Nelson coined the term "hypertext" in 1963[2] while working on Project Xanadu, an ambitious attempt to create a universal document system with bidirectional links and version tracking. Though Xanadu was never fully realized, its ideas influenced everything that followed.

Tim Berners-Lee combined hypertext with the internet in 1989[3], creating the World Wide Web at CERN. His proposal described a distributed system where documents could link to one another across different servers — the architecture we still use today.

Today, hypertext is so fundamental to how we interact with information that we rarely think about it. Every link you click is a small act of hypertext navigation[4].
