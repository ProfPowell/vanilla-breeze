Why Standardized Time Took So Long to Invent

Before 1883, time in the United States was a local affair. Every city of any size kept its own time, calibrated to when the sun was directly overhead at noon. Pittsburgh ran six minutes behind Philadelphia. Chicago ran twenty-eight minutes ahead of Omaha.

What broke the system wasn't astronomy — it was railroads. A train leaving Boston at 9:00 a.m. local time would arrive in Albany running on Albany time, which differed by several minutes. Travelers carried small printed conversion tables.

In 1883, the General Time Convention — an industry body of competing railroads — imposed four time zones on the country by private commercial agreement. Congress would not formalize standard time for another thirty-five years.
