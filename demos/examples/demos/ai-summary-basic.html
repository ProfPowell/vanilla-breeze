Why Standardized Time Took So Long to Invent

Before 1883, time in the United States was a local affair. Every city of any size kept its own time, calibrated to when the sun was directly overhead at noon. Pittsburgh ran six minutes behind Philadelphia. Chicago ran twenty-eight minutes ahead of Omaha. There were over three hundred distinct local times in North America alone, and almost nobody thought this was strange. Time was where you stood.

The railway problem

What broke the system wasn't astronomy or science. It was railroads. A train leaving Boston at 9:00 a.m. local time would arrive in Albany running on Albany time, which differed by several minutes. Travelers carried small printed conversion tables; conductors compared pocket watches. The system worked in the sense that trains generally arrived, but it was a tax on every transaction.

A meeting of railroads

In 1883, the General Time Convention — an industry body of competing railroads — simply imposed a solution on the country. They divided North America into four time zones, drew the lines themselves, and announced that on November 18th every railroad clock would jump to the new system. Congress would not formalize standard time for another thirty-five years.

Why the delay?

The math had been understood since at least the seventeenth century. The technology was widely available decades before 1883. What was missing was sufficient pain. Local time worked fine for local life. It only became intolerable when steam-powered transit collapsed distances faster than coordination could keep up.
