Gear Care

The Complete Gear Care Guide

Keep your equipment performing like new for years, not just seasons.

By Jamie Chen · February 15, 2026 · 8 min read

Why Gear Care Matters

Outdoor gear takes a beating. UV exposure degrades fabrics. Dirt grinds into zippers like sandpaper. Sweat and body oils break down waterproof coatings. Salt from dried perspiration corrodes metal hardware. Left unchecked, these forces can cut the useful life of a $400 tent or a $200 pack in half.

The good news is that proper maintenance is straightforward, requires no specialized skills, and takes surprisingly little time. A few hours of care each season can easily double the functional lifespan of your equipment. That means better performance on the trail and less gear ending up in landfills. At Alpenglow, we designed every product to be maintainable — but even the best engineering needs a little help from its owner.

This guide covers the four pillars of gear longevity: cleaning, waterproofing, storage, and field repair. Whether you’re maintaining a brand-new pack or reviving a well-loved tent, these techniques will keep your equipment performing at its best.

Cleaning Your Gear

Cleaning is the foundation of all gear care. Dirt isn’t just unsightly — it actively damages your equipment. Grit embedded in fabric acts as an abrasive, wearing through fibers with every fold and flex. Residual moisture trapped by dirt promotes mildew growth. And accumulated oils from skin contact degrade DWR (durable water repellent) coatings faster than any amount of rain.

Backpacks and Bags

Backpacks accumulate more body contact residue than any other piece of gear. The shoulder straps, hip belt, and back panel absorb sweat, sunscreen, and insect repellent on every outing. Here’s the proper cleaning sequence:

  1. Empty all pockets and compartments completely. Shake the pack upside down to dislodge crumbs, dirt, and small debris. Use a handheld vacuum on the interior if available.
  2. Remove the frame sheet and hip belt if they’re detachable. These components benefit from individual attention.
  3. Spot clean high-contact areas (straps, back panel, hip belt) with a soft brush and lukewarm water mixed with a small amount of mild soap. A dedicated gear wash like Nikwax Tech Wash is ideal.
  4. For a deep clean, fill a bathtub with lukewarm water and a capful of technical wash. Submerge the pack and gently agitate for 10–15 minutes. Drain and rinse thoroughly with clean water.
  5. Never machine wash structured packs — the agitator can bend internal frames and tear attachment points. Soft daypacks without frames are an exception.
  6. Air dry completely in a well-ventilated area out of direct sunlight. Allow 24–48 hours. A pack stored damp is a pack growing mildew.

Tents and Shelters

Tents face a unique challenge: they must be cleaned without damaging the waterproof coatings on the fly and floor. Aggressive scrubbing or harsh detergents will strip these coatings and compromise weather protection.

  1. Set up the tent at home, ideally on a clean lawn or driveway.
  2. Rinse the entire tent with a garden hose to remove loose dirt and debris.
  3. For stubborn spots, use a tent-specific cleaner (Nikwax Tent & Gear SolarWash is excellent) and a soft sponge. Never use abrasive scrubbers.
  4. Pay special attention to the zipper tracks — dirt in zippers is the number one cause of zipper failure. A soft toothbrush works well here.
  5. Rinse thoroughly and allow to dry completely while pitched. This ensures no moisture gets trapped in folds.
  6. When packing, never fold the tent the same way twice. Repeated creasing along the same lines accelerates coating breakdown at those points. Stuff loosely instead of folding.

Technical Fabrics

Rain jackets, softshell layers, and insulated garments require careful washing to preserve their technical properties. The wrong detergent can destroy a jacket’s breathability or water resistance in a single wash cycle.

  • Always use sport-specific detergents formulated for technical fabrics. Regular laundry detergent leaves residues that clog membrane pores and degrade DWR coatings.
  • Use a front-loading washer only. Top-loading agitator machines can tear seam tape and damage delicate membranes.
  • Never use fabric softener — it coats fibers and completely destroys DWR performance. This is the single most common gear-care mistake.
  • Wash on a gentle cycle with cold or lukewarm water.
  • Tumble dry on low heat for 20 minutes after washing. The heat reactivates DWR coatings. This step is often skipped but makes a significant difference.

Waterproofing and DWR

DWR stands for Durable Water Repellent — a chemical treatment applied to the outer face of waterproof-breathable fabrics. When DWR is working, water beads up and rolls off the surface. When it fails, water spreads out and soaks into the face fabric (called “wetting out”). The membrane underneath still blocks water penetration, but breathability drops dramatically because the saturated outer layer prevents moisture vapor from escaping.

DWR degrades naturally over time through abrasion, UV exposure, contamination from body oils, and repeated washing. The good news: it can be restored. Products like Nikwax TX.Direct or Granger’s Performance Repel work well as wash-in or spray-on treatments.

Quick test: Sprinkle water on the fabric surface. If it beads up into tight droplets, your DWR is fine. If it spreads into a dark wet patch, it’s time to reapply.

Recommended intervals for checking and reapplying DWR treatment by gear type

Rain jacket 10 washes Annually
Tent fly Each season Every 2 years
Backpack 20 uses As needed
Gaiters 5 uses Every 6 months

Storage Best Practices

How you store your gear between trips matters as much as how you maintain it during the season. Improper storage is responsible for more gear failures than trail use. Mildew growth, compression-set insulation, and UV degradation from a sunny garage window can quietly ruin equipment that was in perfect condition when you put it away.

Follow these six rules for long-term gear storage:

  1. Clean everything before storage. Dirt, sweat, and food residues attract mildew, insects, and rodents. Never store dirty gear.
  2. Dry completely — no exceptions. Even slight dampness can produce mildew within days in an enclosed space. If in doubt, air it out for another day.
  3. Store tents loosely stuffed, not rolled. Repeated creasing along fold lines breaks down waterproof coatings. A large mesh storage sack is ideal.
  4. Hang sleeping bags or store uncompressed. Loft is everything with insulation. Compression stuff sacks are for transport only — long-term compression permanently reduces warmth.
  5. Keep gear in a cool, dry, dark location. Avoid garages, attics, and car trunks where temperature extremes and UV exposure accelerate material degradation.
  6. Use breathable storage bags, never plastic. Sealed plastic traps moisture and creates a breeding ground for mildew. Cotton or mesh bags allow airflow.

Field Repairs

Gear failures rarely wait for a convenient time. A torn rain fly at midnight, a broken zipper at a remote trailhead, a delaminating seam on day three of a five-day trip — these situations demand immediate, improvised solutions. Every backcountry traveler should carry a small repair kit and know these four fundamental fixes.

Patching a Tear (Tenacious Tape Method)

Tenacious Tape is the gold standard for field fabric repairs. It bonds permanently to nylon, polyester, and most technical fabrics without sewing.

  1. Clean and dry the area around the tear. Remove any loose threads.
  2. Cut a patch of Tenacious Tape at least 1 inch larger than the tear on all sides. Round the corners — square corners peel up over time.
  3. Apply the patch to the smoother side of the fabric (usually the outer face). Press firmly from center to edges to eliminate air bubbles.
  4. For structural tears under load (pack fabric, tent floors), apply a second patch on the inside for reinforcement.
  5. Allow 24 hours for full adhesion if possible. The bond strengthens significantly overnight.
Fixing a Broken Zipper (Slider Replacement)

Most zipper failures are slider problems, not track problems. A worn slider stops gripping the teeth tightly enough to interlock them. Replacement sliders weigh almost nothing and can save a trip.

  1. Remove the old slider by prying off the top stop (the small metal crimp at the end of the zipper track) with pliers or a knife tip.
  2. Slide the old slider off and thread the new slider onto both sides of the zipper track.
  3. Test the zipper. If it works, crimp a new top stop into place or use a few stitches of heavy thread as a temporary stop.
  4. If the teeth themselves are damaged or missing, the zipper track needs professional replacement — send it to our repair center.
Seam Sealing (When Factory Sealing Fails)

Seam tape can peel after years of use, especially at stress points. Field seam sealing is a temporary fix that can get you through the rest of a trip.

  1. Peel away any loose or bubbling seam tape. Don’t force tape that’s still well-bonded.
  2. Clean the exposed seam with rubbing alcohol if available (hand sanitizer works in a pinch).
  3. Apply seam sealer (Gear Aid Seam Grip is the standard) in a thin, even layer over the exposed stitching.
  4. Allow to cure for at least 4 hours before exposing to moisture. Overnight is better.
Pole Repair (Splint Technique)

A broken tent pole doesn’t have to end your trip. Most quality tents include a pole repair splint in the stuff sack — check yours before you leave home.

  1. Align the broken ends of the pole as closely as possible.
  2. Slide the splint sleeve over the break point, centering it so equal lengths extend on both sides.
  3. Wrap each end of the splint tightly with duct tape or medical tape to prevent the splint from shifting.
  4. If you don’t have a splint, a tent stake can serve as an external splint — tape it firmly along the length of the break.

When to Retire Gear

Even the best-maintained equipment eventually reaches end of life. Continuing to rely on compromised gear in backcountry environments creates genuine safety risks. Watch for these signs:

  • Delamination — waterproof membranes peeling away from face fabrics, creating bubbles or flaking. No amount of DWR reapplication fixes this.
  • Persistent mildew smell that survives thorough washing. Deep mildew has compromised the fabric structure.
  • Fraying at structural seams — shoulder strap attachment points, hip belt junctions, or tent pole sleeves showing thread pull-through.
  • Elastic that won’t recover in cuffs, waistbands, or shock cords. Elastic degradation is irreversible.
  • Zipper tracks with missing or bent teeth that no slider replacement can fix.
  • Foam that has permanently compressed in sleeping pads or pack hip belts, providing noticeably less cushion or support than when new.
  • UV damage — fabric that tears easily when pinched, or coating that flakes off at a touch.

Before you discard old gear, check whether it qualifies for our Lifetime Repair Promise. We can often refurbish equipment that seems beyond help. And when gear truly is done, consider donating it to outdoor education programs that can repurpose materials for teaching repairs and maintenance.
