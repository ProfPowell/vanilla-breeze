Darwin's Voyage

Darwin's voyage on the Beagle lasted nearly five years.

1831–1836. The ship was a Cherokee-class 10-gun brig-sloop commanded by Captain Robert FitzRoy.
During this time he collected specimens from across South America, the Galapagos Islands, and the Pacific. His observations of finch beak variation across the archipelago would later inform his theory of natural selection.

The voyage was not originally planned as a scientific expedition.

FitzRoy wanted a gentleman companion to avoid the loneliness of command. Darwin was recommended by his botany professor John Stevens Henslow.
FitzRoy's primary mission was to chart the coastline of South America and establish a chain of chronometric stations. Darwin's role was informal — he paid his own way and was free to explore at each port of call.

Upon returning to England in 1836, Darwin began sorting his enormous collection of specimens and writing up his geological observations. It would take him another twenty years to publish On the Origin of Species, hesitating until Alfred Russel Wallace independently arrived at a similar theory.
