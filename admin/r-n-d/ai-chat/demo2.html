<ai-chat> — page-aware

Same on-device chat component as demo.html, but with a context attribute pointing at a page region. Its text is folded into the session's system prompt at session creation, so the model can answer questions about the surrounding article without any retrieval plumbing. Inspired by the text-extraction trick in <ai-summary>.

Usage

<ai-chat
  context="#page-content"
  context-label="the article above"
  system="Answer questions using only the page content provided."
  placeholder="Ask about this page…">
</ai-chat>

The context attribute is a CSS selector. On session creation, the component clones the matching element, strips its own UI, normalizes whitespace, and prepends the result to the system prompt under a clear delimiter. If the selected text is large, a warning appears on the context ribbon — Gemini Nano's window is small (~4–6k tokens), so very long pages should be summarized first or chunked.

Why Standardized Time Took So Long to Invent

Before 1883, time in the United States was a local affair. Every city of any size kept its own time, calibrated to when the sun was directly overhead at noon. Pittsburgh ran six minutes behind Philadelphia. Chicago ran twenty-eight minutes ahead of Omaha. There were over three hundred distinct local times in North America alone, and almost nobody thought this was strange. Time was where you stood.

The railway problem

What broke the system wasn't astronomy or science. It was railroads.

A train leaving Boston at 9:00 a.m. local time would arrive in Albany running on Albany time, which differed by several minutes. A traveler buying a connecting ticket in Albany faced a printed schedule based on whose time? The originating city? The destination? The railroad's headquarters?

Different railroads handled this differently. By the 1870s, some major railway terminals displayed multiple clocks — one for each line operating from the station. Passengers carried small printed conversion tables. Conductors compared pocket watches with each other and with the schedule. The system worked, in the sense that trains generally arrived, but it was a tax on every transaction.

Worse, when trains collided — and they did — investigators frequently discovered the trains had been operating on different times entirely, separated by an unmarked transition somewhere along the track.

A meeting of railroads

In 1883, the General Time Convention — an industry body of competing railroads — simply imposed a solution on the country. They divided North America into four time zones, drew the lines themselves, and announced that on November 18th every railroad clock would jump to the new system.

The federal government had nothing to do with this. Congress would not formalize standard time for another thirty-five years, in 1918. The change was implemented entirely by private commercial agreement.

Cities resisted. Boston refused for several years to align city hall with railway time. Detroit kept local solar time until 1900. Religious leaders in some communities argued that "noon" was an act of God determined by the sun, not by industrialists in Chicago. A Cincinnati newspaper editorialized that the sun was no longer to boss the job.

But the railroads kept publishing their schedules in railway time, and gradually everyone else surrendered.

The international version

Around the same time, the British were trying to solve the same problem at a global scale. In 1884, the International Meridian Conference in Washington designated Greenwich as the prime meridian and divided the world into twenty-four hourly time zones. France abstained for twenty-seven years; they preferred the meridian to run through Paris.

What's striking is how recent all this is. People who lived through the Civil War saw America before standard time. People who saw the Wright Brothers fly remembered when their town kept its own clock.

Why the delay?

The interesting question isn't why standard time was invented. It's why it took so long.

The math had been understood since at least the seventeenth century. The technology — reliable mechanical clocks, telegraph synchronization — was widely available decades before 1883. What was missing was sufficient pain. Local time worked fine for local life. It only became intolerable when steam-powered transit collapsed distances faster than coordination could keep up.

The pattern repeats. Standard rail gauges emerged the same way. Standardized screw threads. Shipping containers. Each time the underlying need had existed for generations, but the cost of fragmentation only became unbearable when something else — usually a technology of speed or scale — made the fragmentation expensive in a new way.

We are probably living through several such moments right now and don't recognize them. Some current piece of infrastructure that everyone considers natural and permanent will, fifty years from now, look as quaint as a Pittsburgh businessman setting his watch by the cathedral bell.

Chat with the article

The chips below the ribbon are starter prompts. Picking one sends it immediately. Changing the context selector below resets the session so the new context takes effect.

What changed vs. demo.html

  • New attribute context. A CSS selector. On connectedCallback, the component resolves it relative to document (so it can point at a sibling or ancestor element), then re-resolves it on every reset() in case the page mutated.
  • Text extraction. Same trick as <ai-summary>: clone the target, drop any nested ai-chat shell, take textContent, collapse whitespace.
  • System-prompt fold. The extracted text is appended to the configured system prompt under a fenced [PAGE CONTENT] delimiter. The model sees a single coherent system message; the user-visible system prompt stays editable in the demo controls.
  • Context ribbon. Shows the selector, character count, and a soft warning when the body is large enough that conversation room is at risk. Driven by a single data-context-state attribute.
  • Starter chips. Five canned questions, declared via a child <template data-role="starters"> (one per line). They auto-hide once a message exists, courtesy of :has().

Design notes

  • Single round of context. Page content is included once, in the system prompt. No per-turn injection. That's enough for "summarize / Q&A about a page" — anything more (RAG, multi-doc) belongs in a different component.
  • Lazy. Text extraction runs when the session is created, not on connect. Pages that mutate before the first send are read at the right moment.
  • Token budget honesty. Roughly 1 token ≈ 4 chars. The ribbon warns above ~6000 chars (≈1500 tokens) because Nano's window is small and the user still needs room for chat turns.
  • Same data-attribute discipline. All new state surfaces as data-context-state on the host. CSS handles the visual differences; no .classList juggling.
